The Man of the Millennium for Sri Vaishnavas

Thirumalai Ananthalwan (c. 1053 CE) is revered as one of the greatest exemplars of acharya bhakti in our sampradaya. A direct disciple of Bhagavad Ramanujacharya, he volunteered to serve Lord Venkateswara on the dangerous, forested Tirumala hills when no one else would.

He transformed the neglected shrine into a vibrant center of daily pushpa kainkaryam (flower garland service) and left an eternal legacy through the Ramanuja shrine, his literary works, and the living tradition of his descendants who proudly call themselves Madhurakavi Dāsan.

Early Life & Surrender to Bhagavad Ramanujacharya

Born in 1053 CE at Siruputtur (Kirangur) near Mysore in a pious Yajur Veda family of Baradvaja gotra. His father Kesavacharya named him Ananthan. From a young age he mastered the Vedas and shastras and was immersed in Bhagavata bhakti.

Drawn by the greatness of Bhagavad Ramanujacharya, he surrendered at Srirangam and became his devoted disciple. He came to be known as Ananthalvan because of his intense acharya bhakti (in the same way as other great Alvans).

"He was lost in the ocean of Bhagavad Bhagavata Acharya Bhakti."

— Traditional description

The Divine Call to Tirumala & Pushpa Kainkaryam

During Ramanujacharya’s visit to Tirumala, the temple was neglected and the hills were dense forest. Inspired by Nammalwar’s hymns on the “flower-bedecked Venkata Hills”, Ramanujacharya assembled his 74 disciples and asked who would undertake lifelong daily kainkaryam on the dangerous hills.

All hesitated — except one strong and silent devotee.

"Beloved Acharya! Bestow the blessing of serving the Lord of the Seven Hills on this humble self..."

— Ananthalwan

Ramanujacharya embraced him and declared: “Anantharya, You are truly the man (Aanpillai).”

Ananthalwan Thota

The sacred flower garden he created with his wife. Fragrant flowers for the Lord’s daily garlands. Lord Venkateswara visits it even today.

Ramanujacharya’s Second Visit

Ramanujacharya walked through the lush garden and was overjoyed. He told Ananthalwan: "O Anantharya! Having nursed Thee, I now reap the fruit." — referring to Nammalwar’s pasuram.

Sacred Leelas of Acharya Bhakti

MOST MOVING
The Garland Delay & Madhurakavi Parallel

One day while making the garland, Ananthalwan was delayed. The Lord sent word to come immediately. Ananthalwan refused, saying he would come only when the garland was finished.

The Lord, in pranaya kopa, told him to leave Tirumala. Ananthalwan fearlessly replied:

“I am here not upon Your command but due to my acharya’s command. You have no right to ask me to leave. I would rather disobey Your command than disobey my acharya’s directive. Moreover, You arrived after Sri Varaha Perumal.”

The Lord was greatly pleased and declared that Ananthalwan’s acharya bhakti was equal to that of Madhurakavi Azhvar to Nammazhvar. From that day, he and all his descendants are known as Madhurakavi Dāsan.

The Chin Leela & The Sacred Crowbar

While digging the tank with great zeal, Ananthalwan’s pregnant wife was helping. A young brahmachari offered assistance. In his intense protectiveness of personal kainkaryam, Ananthalwan threw the crowbar. It struck the young man, who disappeared into the sanctum.

When Ananthalwan entered, he saw blood flowing from the chin of Lord Venkateswara’s murti. Realizing it was the Lord Himself, he applied pacchaikarpuram (medicated camphor) and begged forgiveness.

The small mark remains on the Lord’s chin to this day. The crowbar is preserved at the temple entrance as a sacred memento.

Teachings, Literature & Philosophy

Goda Chatusloki

Four beautiful verses on Andal (Goda Devi). Ananthalwan had profound devotion to Andal — once diving into the Srivilliputtur tank searching for remnants of her turmeric.

Ramanuja Chatusloki

Four verses highlighting the four places dearest to Ramanujacharya: Srirangam, Tirumalai, Kanchipuram, and Melkote.

What is a True Sri Vaishnavite? (Ananthalwan’s famous reply)

"A true Sri Vaishnavite is like a crane, like a cock, like salt."

Crane — Vigilant, awaits a true Gnani to surrender to.
Cock — Picks only the good grains (Dravida Veda of Nammalwar).
Salt — Dissolves completely in Bhagavat, Bhaagavata & Acharya kainkaryam; remains humble.
Bhoothatthazhvar’s Pasuram — Beautiful Interpretation

Ananthalwan interpreted the elephant-in-musth pasuram as Ramanujacharya lovingly feeding devotees the essence of Dvayam (the bamboo with two knots), surrounded by Tirumantram and Carama Slokam (the honey), with tears of joy while enjoying Nammalwar.

Eternal Legacy

The Ramanuja Shrine at Tirumala

Ananthalwan’s final sacred gift to pilgrims. The murti was given by Ramanujacharya himself. It is more ancient than several other famous images.

The Ananthalwan Satari

The satari used in the Ramanuja shrine is named after him — parallel to the Sadagopa satari for Nammalwar in the main sanctum.
