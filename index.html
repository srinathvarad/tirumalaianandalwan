THENKALAI ACHARYA

About Thirumalai Anandalwan

Thirumalai Anandalwan (also known as Ananthalwan, Anantharya, or Ananthanpillai) was a great 11th-century Sri Vaishnava Acharya and one of the foremost disciples of Bhagavad Ramanujacharya. He is especially revered in the Thenkalai sampradayam for his complete surrender (prapatti), Acharya Abhimana, and dedicated kainkaryam at Tirumala.

Born in 1053 CE under Chithirai Nakshatra in Siruputtur (Kirangur) near Mysore in the Bharadvaja gotra, he was drawn to the magnetic personality and teachings of Ramanujacharya. When Bhagavad Ramanujacharya called for someone to serve Lord Venkateswara on the difficult Tirumala hills — then a dense forest full of wild animals — Ananthalwan courageously volunteered with full prapatti.

Ramanujacharya embraced him and declared him “Aanpillai” (the worthy and courageous one). From that day, Ananthalwan lived a life of complete dedication to the service of Lord Venkateswara, following the command of his Acharya with unwavering devotion.

LIFE OF DEDICATED KAINKARYAM

Life & Selfless Service at Tirumala

The Call from Ramanujacharya

During one of his visits to Tirumala, Bhagavad Ramanujacharya saw the poor state of the temple and worship. He called an assembly of Acharyas and asked if anyone would undertake the daily service of the Lord on the Hills — growing flowers, maintaining the garden, and ensuring regular kainkaryam. Many hesitated due to the difficult conditions (dense forest, wild animals, malaria). Ananthalwan rose and accepted the responsibility with complete surrender.

Creating the Sacred Garden & Tank

Ananthalwan, along with his wife, cleared the forest area and established a beautiful flower garden (Ananthalwan Thota / Purasai Thota) to offer fresh garlands to Lord Venkateswara every day. He also dug a sacred tank and lake to ensure a perennial supply of water. He named them after his Acharya Ramanujacharya.

The Crowbar Incident (Spot on the Chin)

One of the most moving incidents in his life occurred while he was digging the tank. His pregnant wife was struggling to carry baskets of mud. A young brahmachari (Lord Venkateswara Himself in disguise) offered to help her. In a moment of protective anger, thinking the stranger was interfering with their kainkaryam, Ananthalwan struck him with a crowbar on the chin.

The young man disappeared into the temple. When the priests opened the sanctum, they found blood oozing from the chin of Lord Venkateswara. Overcome with remorse, Ananthalwan applied paccha karpooram (medicated camphor) and prayed for forgiveness. The Lord was pleased with his pure intention and complete dedication.

This incident is commemorated even today:

  • Paccha karpooram is applied daily on the Lord’s chin as prasad.
  • The crowbar is preserved in the temple.
  • The Bhag Savari Utsavam reenacts this episode every year.
DEVOTIONAL LEGEND

The Loving Incident of Purasai Thota

A popular devotional story that reveals the intimate and playful relationship between the Lord and His devotee.

Ananthalwan and his wife worked tirelessly in the garden, growing fragrant flowers and making beautiful garlands for Lord Venkateswara. One night, Lord Srinivasa and Goddess Padmavathi came to the garden to enjoy the flowers. They walked through the pathways, smelling the blossoms and playfully trampling some flowers in their joy.

The next morning, Ananthalwan was shocked to see the garden in disarray. Thinking someone had deliberately destroyed his kainkaryam, he decided to catch the culprit. That night, he hid in the bushes.

When the divine couple returned, Ananthalwan jumped out and caught Goddess Padmavathi (mistaking her for a thief), while Lord Srinivasa escaped. He tied her to a tree and waited the whole night, hoping to catch her husband as well.

The next day, the temple priest came running and informed him that the woman he had tied was none other than Goddess Padmavathi. Ananthalwan was overcome with shame and remorse. He immediately carried Goddess Padmavathi in a flower basket and offered her back to Lord Srinivasa in the sanctum sanctorum.

Because Ananthalwan “offered” Goddess Padmavathi to Lord Srinivasa, he is traditionally revered as the father-in-law (Mamanar) of Lord Venkateswara. This incident is joyfully celebrated every year as the Purasai Thota Utsavam at Tirumala.

LITERARY CONTRIBUTIONS

Literary Works of Ananthalwan

Ananthalwan composed beautiful works that reflect deep Acharya Abhimana and devotion to Andal and Ramanujacharya.

1. Ramanuja Chatusloki

Four verses glorifying Bhagavad Ramanujacharya and the Divya Desams most dear to him, including Tirumala. This work shows Ananthalwan’s profound Acharya Abhimana.

अखिलात्मगुणावासमज्ञानतिमिरापहम् ।
आश्रितानां सुचरणं वन्देऽनन्तार्यदेशिकम् ॥

श्रीमद्रामानुजार्यस्य चरणाम्बुरुहद्वयम् ।
सदुत्तमाङ्गसन्तार्यमनन्तार्यमहं भजे ॥

2. Gotha Chatusloki

Four verses in praise of Andal (Goda Devi), rich in devotional sentiment and philosophical depth. Highly cherished in the Thenkalai tradition.

गोदायै नमस्तुभ्यं वेदान्तार्थप्रकाशिनि ।
यस्याः कृपया लभ्यते भगवद्भक्तिरुत्तमा ॥

THENKALAI IDENTITY

Significance in Thenkalai Tradition

Acharya Abhimana & Prapatti

Ananthalwan lived as the perfect example of Acharya Abhimana and complete dependence on the Acharya. He accepted every command of Ramanujacharya with joy and surrendered his entire life to the service of the Lord at Tirumala.

Madhurakavidasa

Just as Madhurakavi Alvar knew no Lord other than Nammalvar, Ananthalwan declared that he knew no Lord other than his Acharya Ramanujacharya. He proudly called himself Madhurakavidasa. This identity is deeply respected in the Thenkalai sampradayam.

Devotion to Andal

Ananthalwan had extraordinary devotion to Andal. He once dived into the temple tank at Srivilliputtur searching for remnants of the turmeric used by Andal. His Gotha Chatusloki is a beautiful expression of this love.

True Vaishnava

When asked to define a true Srivaishnava, Ananthalwan gave a profound reply: A true Srivaishnava is like a crane (patient), like a cock (discriminating), like salt (self-effacing), and free of ego — always humble and devoted to Bhagavath, Bhaagavatha, and Acharya kainkaryam.

Legacy & Continuing Traditions

Ramanuja Shrine at Tirumala

Ananthalwan installed the earliest known idol of Bhagavad Ramanujacharya at Tirumala, presented to him by Ramanujacharya himself. It remains one of the most important shrines on the Hills.

Ananthalwan Satari

The Satari (representing Nammalwar) in the Ramanuja shrine at Tirumala is known as the Ananthalwan Satari, a unique honor.

Purasai Thota Utsavam

Every year on the day of the incident, Lord Venkateswara visits the garden and honors the sacred tree under which Ananthalwan attained the Lord’s feet on Thiru Adi Pooram.
