THE GREAT ACHARYA

About Thirumalai Anandalwan

Thirumalai Anandalwan (also known as Ananthalwan, Anantharya, or Ananthalwar) was a prominent 11th-century Sri Vaishnava saint and one of the foremost disciples of Bhagavad Ramanujacharya.

Born in Siruputtur (Kirangoor) near Mysore in present-day Karnataka, he was deeply drawn to Ramanuja’s magnetic personality and profound teachings. When Bhagavad Ramanuja called upon his disciples to serve Lord Venkateswara on the challenging Tirumala hills — then a dense forest inhabited by wild animals — Ananthalwan courageously volunteered.

Ramanuja embraced him warmly and declared, “Anantharya, You are truly the man (Aanpillai).” This marked the beginning of his lifelong dedicated service at Tirumala.

Birth & Early Life
Chithirai Nakshatra • Karnataka

Born in 1053 CE in Siruputtur near Mysore. From a young age, he was deeply interested in Vedas and scriptures, eventually becoming a devoted disciple of Ramanujacharya.

Key Title
Aanpillai – The Worthy One

Ramanuja personally acknowledged his courage and dedication by calling him “Aanpillai” when he volunteered to serve at Tirumala despite the hardships of forest life.

Moksha Sthalam
Tirumalai Ananthalwar Thota

He attained eternal service at the sacred garden he himself created on the Tirumala hills on the auspicious day of Thiru Adi Pooram.

LIFE OF DEDICATED SERVICE

Life & Legacy at Tirumala

1
Meeting Bhagavad Ramanujacharya
Early years • Karnataka

Drawn by Ramanuja’s teachings and personality, Ananthalwan became his disciple. He absorbed the philosophy of Vishishtadvaita and the importance of kainkaryam (service).

2
Volunteering for Tirumala Service
Ramanuja’s Call

When Ramanuja sought volunteers to serve Lord Venkateswara on the wild Tirumala hills, Ananthalwan stepped forward without hesitation. Ramanuja embraced him and gave him the affectionate title “Aanpillai”.

3
Creating the Sacred Garden (Thota)
Ananthalwan Thota • Tirumala

Along with his wife, he cleared the forest and established a beautiful flower garden with vakula, patala, punnaga, and shenbaga trees. He also dug a lake named after his guru Ramanuja to support the garden.

4
Temple Reforms & Inscriptions
Epigraphs TT 171, 173, 175

He played a key role in organizing Vaikhanasa Agama worship at Tirumala. His dedication is recorded in several stone inscriptions on the Tirumala hills, highlighting his commitment to Ramanuja’s vision.

5
Attaining Moksha
Thiru Adi Pooram Day

After a life of intense devotion and service, he merged with the Lord at the very garden he had nurtured — Tirumalai Ananthalwar Thota — on the sacred day of Thiru Adi Pooram.

MOST BELOVED LEGEND

The Spot on the Chin

A touching incident that reveals the Lord’s saulabhya (accessibility) and the depth of a devotee’s love

During a severe drought, Ananthalwan and his pregnant wife were digging a well to water the plants in the garden. Exhausted from carrying heavy mud baskets, his wife was struggling.

A young boy appeared and insisted on helping her carry the baskets. Initially reluctant, she eventually accepted his help. When Ananthalwan saw his wife returning much sooner than expected, he inquired and learned about the boy’s assistance.

In a moment of protective anger, thinking it improper for a stranger to help his wife in her condition, Ananthalwan chased the boy away and struck him on the chin with an iron crowbar.

The boy immediately disappeared into the temple sanctum. The next day, when Ananthalwan entered the Garbhagriha, he was devastated to see blood oozing from the chin of Lord Venkateswara Himself.

Overcome with remorse and love, he applied camphor to the Lord’s chin and prayed for forgiveness.

Traditions That Continue to This Day

Camphor on the Chin

To this day, priests apply paccha karpooram (camphor) to Lord Venkateswara’s chin as both a beauty mark and prasad, commemorating Ananthalwan’s loving remorse.

The Crowbar

The iron crowbar used in the incident is preserved and displayed in the mandapam near the gopuram entrance as a sacred reminder of the Lord’s playful interaction with His devotee.

Bhag Savari Utsavam

This incident is beautifully reenacted every year during the Bhag Savari Utsavam at Tirumala, where Lord Malayappa is taken in a procession depicting the divine play.

LITERARY CONTRIBUTIONS

Works of Ananthalwan

Ramanuja Chatusloki

A profound four-verse composition highlighting how Srirangam, Tirumala Hills, Kanchipuram, and Melkote were especially dear to Bhagavad Ramanujacharya.

Gotha Chatusloki

A deeply moving composition rich in devotion, imagery, and philosophical depth, glorifying the divine qualities of Andal (Goda Devi).

Venkatachala Itihasamala

An important historical chronicle of Tirupati (Tirumala), comparable to the Koil Olugu of Srirangam. It preserves valuable traditional accounts of the temple’s history and Ramanuja’s contributions.

This work was chronicled with the involvement of Anantha Suri, reflecting Ananthalwan’s deep connection to Tirumala’s heritage.

Why Thirumalai Anandalwan Matters Today

His life exemplifies unwavering kainkaryam (devotional service), courage in the face of hardship, and the beautiful relationship between the Lord and His devotee — where even apparent mistakes become opportunities for divine grace and deeper love.

The garden he created and the traditions he helped establish continue to enrich the spiritual experience of millions of pilgrims at Tirumala even after nearly a thousand years.
